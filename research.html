Research

Studying aviation as a technical and public system.

My research begins with real planning questions: where can new operations fit, what conditions make them safe, who benefits, and what institutions are needed to make the system work?

Doctoral research

Advanced Air Mobility at large hub airports

My PhD research in Aviation and Aerospace Technology, within Purdue’s Computational Science and Engineering track, develops a two-stage decision-support framework for Advanced Air Mobility integration at U.S. large hub airports. The first stage screens locations and operating scenarios for safety feasibility. The second evaluates viable alternatives across economic viability, operational efficiency, natural resource conservation, and social responsibility.

The work integrates FAA airport design and airspace guidance, spatial constraints, scenario analysis, and multi-criteria decision methods to identify where, when, and under what conditions AAM integration may be feasible.

Current methods: ArcGIS Pro, spatial screening, AHP, TOPSIS, sensitivity analysis, and scenario comparison.
Global safety

Institutional capacity, conflict, and aviation safety

Aviation safety depends on more than aircraft technology and operator behavior. National institutions shape regulatory capacity, infrastructure quality, oversight, reporting, and system resilience. My global aviation safety research examines how institutional capacity, conflict intensity, state fragility, and economic conditions relate to accident occurrence across countries and over time.

This stream connects quantitative safety analysis with the realities of resource-constrained and conflict-affected aviation systems, informed by prior work in airworthiness oversight, safety management, quality, security, and humanitarian aviation operations.

Current methods: panel count models, zero-inflated negative binomial models, Bayesian analysis, spatial visualization, and robustness testing.
Human–automation

Cognitive challenges in UAS-AAM integration

Emerging aviation systems change the allocation of attention, authority, and responsibility between people and automation. Collaborative work in this area examines cognitive and human-automation challenges that may affect safe integration of uncrewed aircraft and Advanced Air Mobility operations.

Current approach: thematic review and interdisciplinary synthesis across aviation human factors, automation, and operational integration.
Spatial decisions

GIS and transparent trade-off analysis

Many aviation planning problems are spatially uneven. Safety constraints, environmental sensitivity, infrastructure access, social exposure, and operational demand vary across locations. I use GIS and spatial multi-criteria decision analysis to make these differences visible and to support transparent comparison among alternatives.

My approach emphasizes traceable criteria, clearly defined exclusion rules, normalized indicators, uncertainty analysis, and maps that communicate both feasibility and trade-offs.

Applications: vertiport siting, airport integration, noise exposure, flood risk, environmental buffers, accessibility, and infrastructure readiness.
Transport policy

Airports as metropolitan mobility nodes

Emerging aviation technologies will matter only when they create value beyond the aircraft. My work treats airports as metropolitan mobility nodes and asks whether airside efficiency translates into meaningful accessibility, resilience, equity, and public benefit.

This perspective connects airport planning, aviation regulation, ground transportation, local governance, and infrastructure investment.

Research should help make decisions clearer.

My goal is to produce work that is methodologically careful and useful to the people planning, regulating, and operating aviation systems.
